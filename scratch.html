Cereal History

The history of breakfast cereal begins in the late 19th century as a response to the need for simple, health-focused foods in America. It was initially developed by Dr. John Harvey Kellogg and his brother Will Keith Kellogg, who sought to create a nutritious vegetarian option for patients at their health spa. They introduced cornflakes in 1894, which became a sensation. Around the same time, Charles W. Post created Grape-Nuts, another early cereal success. The industry boomed as manufacturers added sugar and creative marketing to appeal to children and families. Over the 20th century, cereals diversified, with brands introducing flavors, shapes, and fortified options, solidifying their place as a breakfast staple worldwide.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 


Cereal Production

Cereal production begins with sourcing grains such as corn, wheat, oats, or rice, which are milled into flour or other base ingredients. These are mixed with water and other components like sweeteners, flavorings, and vitamins. The mixture is then shaped using processes like extrusion, where dough is pushed through molds to form flakes, loops, or puffs. The cereal is cooked, often baked or toasted, to achieve the desired texture. Sugary coatings or flavor dustings may be added before packaging. Stringent quality control ensures consistency and safety throughout production, and the final product is packed into boxes and shipped to retailers, ready to brighten breakfast tables.
